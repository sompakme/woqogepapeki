Early Life and Career Beginnings

Juvenile, whose real name is Terius Gray, was born on March 25, 1975, in New Orleans, Louisiana. Growing up in the Magnolia Projects of New Orleans, he developed a deep passion for music. His early exposure to the vibrant local music scene fueled his ambition to pursue a career in the industry.

To kick-start his career, Juvenile signed a record deal with Warlock Records in 1995 and released his debut album, “Being Myself.” Although it didn’t make a significant impact nationally, it laid the foundation for his subsequent success. The album caught the attention of Cash Money Records, which offered him a better opportunity to showcase his talent.

With the newfound support of Cash Money Records, Juvenile’s career gained momentum. He collaborated with fellow rap artists and producers who would become instrumental in shaping his distinctive sound. His early albums, such as “Solja Rags” (1997), laid the groundwork for his rise to fame and marked the beginning of his fruitful partnership with Cash Money Records’ in-house producer, Mannie Fresh.

While Juvenile’s early life and career beginnings set the stage for his future success, it was his debut album under Cash Money Records, “400 Degreez,” that catapulted him into the mainstream spotlight. This breakthrough album became a cultural phenomenon, achieving 4 times Platinum certification and solidifying Juvenile’s status as a prominent figure in the hip-hop industry.

Rise to Fame with Cash Money Records

After signing with Cash Money Records, Juvenile’s career took a significant turn, propelling him into the spotlight of the music industry. His collaboration with Mannie Fresh, the in-house producer of Cash Money, proved to be a winning combination, resulting in a string of successful projects.

One of the pivotal moments in Juvenile’s career came with the release of his breakthrough album, “400 Degreez,” in 1998. This album, featuring popular tracks like “Back That Azz Up” and “Ha,” not only achieved massive commercial success but also solidified Juvenile’s status as a prominent figure in the rap scene. With its unique blend of New Orleans bounce and gritty lyricism, “400 Degreez” resonated with audiences nationwide, ultimately earning the album a 4 times Platinum certification by the RIAA.

In addition to his solo success, Juvenile’s collaboration with fellow Cash Money Records artists, including Lil Wayne, further contributed to his rise to fame. The formation of the hip-hop group The Hot Boys, consisting of Juvenile, Lil Wayne, B.G., and Turk, brought further recognition and established Juvenile as a force to be reckoned with in the industry.
